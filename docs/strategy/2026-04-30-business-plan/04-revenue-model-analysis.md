## TL;DR — Recommended primary + secondary

**Primary: Productized service packages with a paid pilot funnel into a monthly retainer.** Three named, fixed-scope packages (Audit, Build Sprint, SaaS Replacement) sit on the homepage as the public price list. Every Build Sprint is designed to convert into a $2,000–$3,500/mo Operations Retainer once the first system is live. This is hybrid model #4 + #3 — productized at the front, recurring at the back. It is the SF AI Institute pattern, sharpened.

**Secondary: SaaS spinoff (WikiFold pattern).** A single internal tool — the one used most across client engagements — gets productized as a $79–$199/mo SaaS sold first to existing clients, then to the open market. This is the asymmetric-upside hedge against consulting commoditization and the only model on the list with genuine 10x potential without hiring.

**Explicitly rejected:** hourly consulting (#1), pure equity / rev-share (#6), training cohorts as a primary revenue line (#8). Reasons in the rejected-models section.

**Headline pricing to publish on the site (today):**

- Diagnostic Audit — **$3,500** flat, two weeks, written report + recorded walkthrough
- Build Sprint — **$18,000** flat, four weeks, one shipped system, source code transferred
- SaaS Replacement — **$35,000–$55,000** flat, 6–10 weeks, scoped per audit
- Operations Retainer — **$2,500/mo** (12-month) or **$3,000/mo** (month-to-month), capped hours, named humans
- Founder office hours (existing clients only) — **$450/hr** as published rate, not a primary product

## Detailed evaluation

### 1. Hourly consulting

Highest hourly margin of any model, instant cash, zero overhead — but every dollar requires a fresh hour. Senior AI consultants charge $150–$450/hr; $250/hr is realistic for a 25-year engineer solo. Two founders billing 25 hr/wk each cap out at ~$13K/wk — higher rates raise the ceiling but don't break it. This is the canonical 10x trap Hardy explicitly names: hours can't be spent on assets that compound. Brand fit is weak — hourly creates clock-watching and scope haggling, and opaque rates violate the published-pricing principle. **Verdict:** useful as a published-but-rarely-used rate for existing-client emergencies. Not the engine.

### 2. Fixed-scope project pricing

With templates, gross margin lands at 60–75% on a four-week build. A $15K–$20K project at one founder's full-time effort = $3,750–$5,000/wk per founder — 1.5–2x the hourly equivalent. Scalability is linear with mild template-leverage (each project compresses the next). Capital intensity near-zero. Sullivan fit is partial: funds the business but doesn't compound unless engineered as a gateway into recurring. Brand fit is excellent — published dollar amounts are the strongest Playbook B signal, and only SF AI Institute and Lindy among 10 scanned competitors show real prices. Industry SMB AI build benchmark: $5K–$50K. **Verdict:** foundational layer of the primary recommendation.

### 3. Monthly retainer

70–85% gross margin from month 4+, billed monthly in advance, predictable cash. Sub-linear scaling — 8–12 active retainers at $2,500–$3,500/mo = $20K–$42K/mo for a 2-person shop ($250K–$500K ARR floor, the right order of magnitude). Capital intensity low. SMB services retainers churn 8–15%/yr if delivery is solid; the failure mode is the "what did we pay for this month" complaint, countered only by concrete monthly deliverables. Sullivan fit is strong — recurring is the spine of compounding and the literal "second sale" Hardy recommends. Brand fit is strong if framed as "named humans on call," risky if framed as "AI-as-a-service." Benchmarks: SF AI Institute "from $2,500/mo," Purple Horizons "starts at $1,000/mo," fractional CTO/CIO $5K–$15K/mo, AI Automation Agency ~$450/mo support. **The $2,500–$3,500/mo band is the SMB sweet spot.** **Verdict:** secondary engine — back-end of the productized funnel.

### 4. Productized service packages

Best unit economics of any consulting model: naming and fixing scope eliminates 30–40% of the time lost to discovery, scope creep, and proposals. Gross margin 65–80% once templates exist. Cash in 14–30 days. Scalability is linear with stronger template-leverage than #2 because clients self-select into the named SKU — the funnel pre-qualifies. Capital intensity low–medium (build the scaffolding once, run the play). Sullivan fit is good when explicitly designed as a funnel into #3 — Hardy's "self-multiplying structure" is exactly the productized → retainer ladder. **Best brand fit of any model** — named packages with public prices is canonical Playbook B and structurally anti-sales (the package answers questions before they're asked). SF AI Institute is the only scanned competitor with public productized packages. **Verdict:** primary engine.

### 5. Subscription tiers

75–90% gross margin at scale, but "at scale" is the trap. With two founders and high-touch SLAs, the first ~30 subscribers are still founder-bound — tiers only free founders if delivery is partly automated, which is a year-2 capability at earliest. Capital intensity medium. Churn 10–20%/yr typical for high-touch SMB; lower tiers churn 3–5x faster than higher. Sullivan fit is sound in theory, premature in 2026. Brand fit is risky — Bronze/Silver/Gold is the canonical agency template Playbook B rejects. Honest tiers (Solo / Team / Multi-location) could work but need 20+ case studies first. **Verdict:** defer 12–18 months; re-evaluate at 8 paying retainers.

### 6. Equity / rev-share / outcome-based

Zero up-front cash. Variable upside. Median outcome in SMB advisory: equity worth nothing. Asymmetric in theory; in practice requires deal sourcing and legal infrastructure GVC doesn't have. Looks capital-light but has the highest opportunity cost of any model — every equity hour is an hour not billable. Locked and illiquid. **Anti-fit on Sullivan/Hardy** — compounding requires *diversified, recurring* revenue, not concentrated illiquid bets. Equity is year-5+ at earliest. Brand fit weak: outcome-based pricing requires sales theater ("what's the upside? what's the baseline?") that violates the anti-sales tone. **Verdict:** reject. Maybe one warrant per year for a strategic client. Never the model.

### 7. SaaS spinoff

70–85% gross margin at scale, fully recurring. Cash predictable but small until customer count crosses ~200. **The only model on the list with genuine non-linear scaling** — $99/mo × 1,000 customers = $1.2M ARR with two founders and one part-time support role. That is the Sullivan endgame. Capital intensity is high up-front: 6–9 months before meaningful revenue. SMB SaaS churn norm 5–8%/mo MRR early, 2–4%/mo for stable products. **Best Sullivan/Hardy fit on the list** — literal productization of the consulting IP. Brand fit strong if the SaaS stays plain-language and operator-shaped. Benchmarks: Lindy $49.99–$59.99/mo, AppSumo SMB $39–$99/mo, niche operator SaaS $79–$199/mo. **Verdict:** secondary engine. Pick one tool from the consulting work that solves a recurring SMB pain. Single $99/mo tier. Cap founder time at ~20% in year 1.

### 8. Training / cohort courses

80–90% gross margin per cohort, lumpy cash. Teaching is real labor: a 4-week cohort at 10 students × $1,500 = $15K but consumes 40+ founder-hours. Cohort-bound until you build a self-paced product (which is a different business). Capital intensity medium. Decent as a side product that turns content into revenue; bad as primary — cohorts cap at founder bandwidth and "teach you to do it" fights "we do it" positioning. The brand can't lead with both. **Verdict:** year 2+ side product. Possibly one paid cohort late 2026 as a marketing instrument that pays for itself.

## Recommendation

### Primary — Productized packages → Operations Retainer funnel

The engine is three named packages on the homepage with public prices. Every package is engineered to convert into a $2,500–$3,000/mo retainer once delivery is complete. This is the SF AI Institute pattern — but with a build-sprint anchor in the middle that SFAII doesn't publish, and with named humans on the engagement that nobody else has.

**Why this wins:**

1. **Plays directly into the brand.** Public prices + named packages + named humans = Playbook B. No model on the list scores higher on brand fit.
2. **Funds the engine while pointing at compounding.** Productized projects pay this month's bills. The retainer back-end is the first compounding layer.
3. **Matches the validated benchmark.** SF AI Institute proves SMBs will pay $2,500/mo for AI ops support. Purple Horizons proves $1,000/mo is the floor. The middle band is uncrowded and tonally right.
4. **Two founders, two roles.** Matt builds. Melissa runs the operating cadence on the retainer. The package structure makes role assignment unambiguous.

### Secondary — SaaS spinoff (one tool, $99/mo)

After 8–12 build sprints, one internal tool will appear repeatedly across client engagements. That tool gets productized in Q3 2026 at a single $99/mo tier, sold first to existing retainer clients (zero CAC), then to the open market.

**Why secondary, not primary:** SaaS is the only true 10x model on the list, but it requires 6–9 months of capital and attention before producing revenue. If GVC builds it before having a retainer book, the consulting business starves. The right sequence is *retainer book first, SaaS second.*

The candidate tool is most likely a workflow/agent orchestrator — the thing GVC has built six times across clients. WikiFold is one example of how this is structured.

### Models we will NOT pursue (and why)

- **Hourly consulting (#1).** Pure 2x trap. Used only as an internal-only published rate ($450/hr) for existing-client emergencies, never as a customer acquisition product.
- **Pure equity / rev-share (#6).** Wrong stage. Concentrates risk, eliminates diversification, requires sales theater that contradicts the brand. Revisit in 3+ years if at all.
- **Subscription tiers (#5)** *as a launch product.* Premature without 20+ case studies. Re-evaluate at 8 retainer customers.
- **Cohort courses (#8) as primary.** Brand-conflicting if it leads. Acceptable as a Q4 2026 side experiment that funds itself.

## Stress tests

### What if churn is 2x worse than assumed?

Assume retainer churn is 30%/yr instead of 15%. The retainer book then needs net-new sales every 3.3 months instead of every 6.7 months — roughly double the inbound demand requirement. The recommendation **survives** because:

1. The productized package layer still produces standalone revenue regardless of retainer churn.
2. Higher churn signals delivery quality issues, which is fixable, not structural.
3. SaaS spinoff (#7) is a partial hedge — SaaS churn is independent of consulting churn.

But if retainer churn exceeds 30%/yr after 6 months of operation, the diagnosis is *the retainer offer is wrong* (not specific enough, not delivering monthly value, named humans not actually present), not *the model is wrong.* Fix the offer. Do not abandon the model.

### What if founders need to hire a third person sooner than planned?

A third hire at $9–12K/mo all-in raises the monthly burn floor by ~$10K. To absorb that, the retainer book needs to add ~4 customers ($2,500/mo × 4 = $10K). That's roughly one quarter of growth at the planned acquisition rate.

The recommendation **survives** because:

1. Productized packages produce the cash to fund the hire (one Build Sprint = ~2 months of a third person's salary).
2. The third hire is most useful as a delivery operator, not a salesperson — they directly expand retainer capacity, paying themselves back.
3. The brand positioning ("there are two of us, and that's the point") needs honest revision when this happens. Plan now for the language: "We grew to three. The named humans you talk to are still us."

The risk is hiring a salesperson before a delivery operator. **Do not.** That breaks both the brand and the unit economics.

### What if AI commoditizes implementation? (the @ZoidbergCash counter-argument)

This is the most serious threat. If GPT-7-class models in 2027 let any SMB owner self-serve an automation, the Build Sprint product collapses.

The recommendation **partially survives:**

1. **Audit and retainer products survive longer than build products.** Knowing *what to automate*, *in what order*, and *whether AI is the wrong answer* — that's domain judgment, not implementation skill. The audit ($3,500) and retainer ($2,500/mo) hold.
2. **Build sprints commoditize first.** Expect the $18K sprint to compress toward $9K and then toward $4K within 24 months. Plan the SaaS spinoff (#7) before that compression hits, so the recurring base is already standing.
3. **The hospitality vertical wedge is a partial moat.** Generic AI tools can do generic things. AI tuned to a Friday dinner rush, scheduled around a chef's actual workflow, that survives a credit-card terminal outage — that's operational knowledge, not model knowledge. Melissa is the moat.
4. **Brand voice is durable.** When implementation commoditizes, trust becomes the only differentiator. Plain-language, anti-hype, fixed prices, named humans — that brand is *more* valuable when AI floods the market with generic offerings.

The honest read: if AI implementation fully commoditizes, GVC becomes a SaaS company (via the #7 spinoff) plus a high-touch retainer practice for the SMBs that still want a human. The consulting business contracts but doesn't die. The SaaS bet is the insurance policy.
