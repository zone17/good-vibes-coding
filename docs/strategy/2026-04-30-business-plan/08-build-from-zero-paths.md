## TL;DR — recommended primary path

**Primary: Path A → C transition.** Spend 12 months executing Path A (two-person, productized, hospitality-anchored) to hit ~$500–750K bootstrapped revenue at 70%+ margin, while hardening WikiFold as the internal tool that runs the consulting practice. By month 12–18, if WikiFold (or a sibling product) shows organic pull from non-clients, fork it into Path C as a self-funded SaaS spinoff with consulting cash paying the runway. This sequence is uniquely available to GVC because (a) Matt can build product without raising, (b) Mel's hospitality network is the highest-conviction beachhead, and (c) productized consulting is the only path that respects Sullivan/Hardy "Unique Ability" without forcing a 30-person org Matt and Mel don't want to run.

**Credible alternative: Path B.** If, by month 12, demand outstrips two-person capacity by 3x *and* Mel discovers she enjoys leading a hospitality practice, scale to 5–7 people. Safer, less asymmetric, caps at "boutique agency" — not 10x.

**Reject Path D from zero.** Methodology-license is a *post-success* play. Without 30+ shipped engagements and a brand bigger than the founders, there is no IP to license. Revisit at Year 3+.

## Path A — Stay 2-person, productize

**Shape.** Premium, capacity-bound, fixed-scope consultancy. Three or four named tiers in plain language: **Audit ($4.5K, 2 weeks)**, **Pilot Build ($18K, 4–6 weeks)**, **Ops Retainer ($3K/mo)**, **Quarterly Tune-Up ($6K)**. Two humans on every engagement, hospitality-vertical-anchored, expanding to adjacent service SMB. Founders are the product. 15–20 engagements/year, 2–3 active retainers steady-state. Comparables: Patrick McKenzie / Kalzumeus, Paul Jarvis "Company of One," Jonathan Stark.

**Sullivan/Hardy fit.** Strong on Self-Multiplying via packaging + fractional Whos; weak on raw 10x. Matt's UA = systems architecture + judgment partner. Mel's UA = operational diagnosis + trust. At 2-person capacity / 70% margin, $1.2–1.8M revenue is the realistic ceiling — life-changing personal income (~$350–600K each) but a 2x lifestyle business, not 10x. **Honest read:** if "abundance" means time, autonomy, geographic flexibility, and high personal income — A delivers cleanly. If "abundance" means a 10x company with enterprise value to sell — A does not.

**Capital.** Bootstrap, no debt, no equity. ~$15–25K working capital (LLC, insurance, accounting, templates, paid-traffic experiments). Fundable from one engagement.

**Timeline.** First revenue Q2 2026. $500K ARR Month 9–12. $1M ARR Month 18–24. $2M ARR likely never under A. End-state Year 3–5: $1.2–1.8M revenue, 70% margin, founders ~30 hr/wk, 8 weeks off/year.

**Whos.** Fractional bookkeeper/CPA ($300–600/mo, M0–3); paralegal-as-a-service ($200/mo, M0–3); marketing/content Who ($2–3K/mo, M3–6); paid ads / outbound Who ($1.5K/mo + spend, M6–9); EA/scheduling Who ($1.5–2K/mo, M9–12); designer-as-a-service (project-based, M12+). Steady-state fractional spend: **$5–8K/mo = 5–8% of revenue**. No FT hires, no W-2 risk, no agency overhead.

**Risk.** Founder-as-product (one illness compresses revenue); pipeline ceiling at Mel's network's edge; "two people" perceived as risk not feature; commoditization in 24–36 months — the moat *is* the founders, no IP layer; Mel-dependency (operator-engineer wedge collapses if she steps back).

**Founder lifestyle.** Matt 35–40 hr/wk steady, ~50 during pilots; high geographic flexibility. Mel 30–35 hr/wk concentrated in client-facing/discovery; very high flexibility. Optionality very high — can take 4–6 weeks off, scale down to 1 retainer/month, pivot to C with no org disruption.

**Walk-away value.** Low-to-moderate. 2-person productized consultancy with founder-as-product + no contracted recurring revenue has minimal acquisition value. Realistic exit: $300–800K to a regional agency wanting Mel's network and WikiFold IP. A productized SaaS layer (Path C) is the only mechanism that creates real walk-away value from this base.

**Decision points.** M6: 4+ paid engagements without Mel personally introducing each one? If no — paid acquisition broken; niche harder or revisit B. M12: any internal tool showing organic pull from non-clients? If yes — A→C is live. M24: founders <40 hr/wk and earning >$300K each? If yes — A succeeding, do not scale. If no — escalate to B or accept lower income.

**Sullivan/Hardy abundance compatibility.** High. Highest of the four on time-and-energy abundance; lowest on financial-asset abundance. Best fit for "we built a beautiful small thing and live well."

## Path B — Scale to small expert team

**Shape.** Boutique 5–9 person AI implementation firm, two named verticals: **Hospitality** (Mel as practice lead) and **Professional Services / Trades** (Matt as practice lead + a vertical specialist). Matt = CTO/Head of Engineering; Mel = COO/Head of Hospitality. ~30–45 engagements/year, multiple parallel pilots. Comparables: early Sierra (pre-funding), Thoughtbot, Pivotal Labs, services-era 37signals.

**Sullivan/Hardy fit.** Mid on Self-Multiplying, mid-to-high on 10x. Team of 7 done well: $2.5–4M revenue at 35–45% margin (lower than A — payroll is the cost). Closer to 10x trajectory than A *if* founders genuinely enjoy managing. The risk: Matt and Mel become managers, not practitioners. **Honest read:** B works only if at least one founder has Unique Ability in team-building. From the existing materials — engineer + operator pairing, "two humans" as the wedge, "we answer our own emails" — neither founder appears wired for org-building joy. Forcing B in service of 10x economics is the canonical Sullivan/Hardy trap.

**Capital.** Bootstrap-plus. ~$80–150K working capital for 2-month payroll buffer on first hires. Either retained from Path A revenue (recommended) or SBA 7(a) loan ($100K, ~7-yr, 10–11% as of Apr 2026). No equity unless growth gets ahead of cash flow at Year 2.

**Timeline.** First revenue Q2 2026. First hires M6–9 (vertical specialist + ops/PM Who). $500K ARR M9–12. $2M ARR M24–30. End-state Year 3–5: $3–5M revenue, 35–40% margin, 7–9 people, founders 45–55 hr/wk.

**Whos.** M6: hospitality vertical specialist (FT, $90–110K loaded) + ops/PM Who. M9: junior engineer (Matt's apprentice, $90–110K). M12: marketing Who (fractional → FT). M18: second vertical specialist + senior engineer to de-risk Matt ($130–160K). M24: sales/BD only if pipeline can't be founder-fed. Total payroll by M24: ~$700–900K. Requires consistent $2M+ revenue to stay healthy.

**Risk.** Founder-as-manager mismatch (most likely failure mode); margin compression as every hire dilutes the operator-engineer wedge — clients hired Matt and Mel, get a 2-yr-experience associate; cultural homogenization (Pivotal solved with intense expensive culture); commoditization risk shared with A but worse — higher fixed costs to defend; first-hire failure eats 6 months of founder energy.

**Founder lifestyle.** Matt 50–55 hr/wk, mix of architecture, hiring, 1:1s; moderate flexibility (team rituals require sync time). Mel 45–55 hr/wk, vertical practice + external relationships. Optionality moderate — vacation possible but pivoting away requires 6–12 month wind-down.

**Walk-away value.** Moderate. Profitable 7-person consultancy with named verticals + recurring retainers sells for 0.8–1.5x revenue (or 3–5x SDE) to regional agency or rollup. At $3M revenue / $600K SDE: **$1.8–3M exit**. Founder still likely required 12–24 months post-sale.

**Decision points.** M6: first non-founder hire shipping client work without Matt or Mel rebuilding? If no — pause hiring. M12: gross margin >35% and NPS still founder-tier? If no — over-scaled. M24: defined path to <40 hr/wk personal load? If no — B has trapped founders in the 2x execution role they were trying to escape.

**Sullivan/Hardy abundance compatibility.** Mid. Path B can deliver financial abundance and clean walk-away value but trades time and Unique-Ability density. Most viable as a Path A → B *escalation* if demand forces it, never as starting choice.

## Path C — SaaS spinoff from internal tooling

**Shape.** GVC continues consulting at Path A intensity for 12–18 months. In parallel, Matt productizes WikiFold (or whichever internal tool gets pull) into hosted SaaS targeting the same Dana persona. Wedge: *"the AI knowledge base your team will actually update, because it updates itself."* Pricing $99–299/mo, self-serve, freemium top-of-funnel. By Year 3, mix: 40% consulting / 60% SaaS. By Year 5, consulting becomes a high-priced selective channel; SaaS is the cash engine. Comparables: Basecamp from 37signals consulting; Linear founders consulted before building; original Stripe started as Collison consulting.

**Sullivan/Hardy fit.** Highest of the four on 10x; high on Self-Multiplying. SaaS economics break the linear-revenue-per-hour cap. Once at PMF, founders' UA concentrates on product judgment and brand — exactly where Matt (eng/architecture) and Mel (operator empathy → product roadmap) shine. The textbook Sullivan service-to-product play. The trap: 18 months of mid-development drag where consulting must subsidize product without burning founders out. Most service-to-SaaS attempts fail here.

**Capital.** Bootstrap if disciplined; possibly $250–500K SBA loan or revenue-based financing if accelerating. No equity unless PMF triggers a real growth-capital decision Year 2–3. **WikiFold is already built** — the unique advantage GVC has versus most aspirants. Capital goes to (a) hosting/infra, (b) one product engineer to free Matt for architecture, (c) marketing.

**Timeline.** Consulting revenue Q2 2026. First SaaS revenue M6–9. $500K combined ARR M12. **$500K SaaS-only ARR M24–30** (the hard milestone). $2M combined ARR M30–36. $2M SaaS-only M42–54. End-state Year 5: $3–8M ARR, 60–80% from SaaS, 70%+ gross margin, founders in product/strategy roles.

**Whos.** M0–6: same fractional Whos as Path A (consulting funds everything). M9: product engineer FT (frees Matt from feature work). M12: customer success / community Who fractional. M18: growth marketer fractional → FT. M24: senior product engineer + designer FT. M30: DevRel/content lead. By M30: ~5 FTE — markedly leaner than B because SaaS doesn't require humans-per-engagement.

**Risk.** WikiFold doesn't have PMF as SaaS — internal-tool-to-SaaS conversion fails most often because the internal tool was built for one team's idiosyncrasies (mitigation: 5+ paying non-GVC customers before any major commitment); consulting starves the product (mitigation: deliberate calendaring + first product engineer by M9); capital insufficiency — bootstrap SaaS to $1M ARR takes 36+ months, founders run out of patience (mitigation: RBF at M18–24 if metrics support); founder split — Mel is operator-of-businesses, not operator-of-software-product (if Mel disengages from product, GVC loses operator wedge in both arms); Anthropic/OpenAI/vertical SaaS bundles a competing knowledge feature within 24 months (mitigation: lean into "AI for SMBs without an IT team," not technical sophistication).

**Founder lifestyle.** Matt: Year 1 brutal, 55–65 hr/wk consulting + product. Year 2 stabilizes 45–50. Year 3 if successful: 35–40 on product strategy and architecture review. Mel: Year 1 manageable — Path A workload + product input. Year 2: client work scales down, customer voice / product partnership scales up. Optionality low Year 1, moderate Year 2, very high Year 3+ once SaaS dominates.

**Walk-away value.** Highest of the four. SaaS at $2M ARR / 70% margin / LTV/CAC > 3 sells for 4–8x ARR — **$8–16M acquisition**. At $5M ARR: **$20–40M**. Only path with genuine 10x exit math.

**Decision points.** M6: WikiFold (or chosen product) used by 3+ non-GVC orgs who pay or would credibly pay? If no — kill SaaS thesis early; commit to A or B. M12: $5K+ MRR and 5+ paying customers acquired without Matt's personal involvement? If yes — hire first product engineer. If no — wedge is consulting-bundled, not standalone. M24: SaaS gross margin >70%, payback <18mo, churn <5%/mo? If yes — consider scaling capital. If no — keep SaaS as profit center, don't grow.

**Sullivan/Hardy abundance compatibility.** Highest on financial abundance; mid on time abundance during years 1–2. Once over the hump, C delivers the cleanest "Self-Multiplying Company" — software multiplies value while founders multiply judgment.

## Path D — Methodology license / franchise / cohort

**Shape.** GVC codifies the implementation playbook into licensable IP and trains other consultants/operators. Three sub-flavors: **Franchise** (regional GVC-branded shops, royalty + standards model — Strategic Coach licensing), **Cohort** (8–12 wk paid program for indie consultants @ $5–15K/seat, 4 cohorts/year, networked alumni — Hampton, On Deck), **Network/marketplace** (GVC vouches for vetted implementers; revenue share or seat fee). Core asset is the methodology itself + brand reputational capital with SMB owners.

**Sullivan/Hardy fit.** Highest on Self-Multiplying *if it works*; lowest probability of working from a build-from-zero base. Strategic Coach itself is the platonic Path D — Sullivan codified his own methodology after ~15 years and thousands of engagements. GVC has neither in April 2026. The paradox: D is the most Sullivan-aligned end-state, but premature execution destroys the methodology's credibility.

**Capital.** $50–150K bootstrap or low-equity. Course platform (Maven, Circle), community infra, marketing. Franchising adds $30K+ legal for FDD and state-by-state registration. Cohort: lower legal, high content production cost.

**Timeline (if started Year 0, not recommended).** First revenue M12 if cohort-only. $500K ARR M24+ (100 students/year @ $5K). $2M ARR Year 4+. End-state highly variable: $1–10M depending on community/network depth.

**Timeline (recommended — Year 3+ start, after A or A→C maturity).** Year 3: first cohort, $250K. Year 4: $750K methodology revenue. Year 5: $1.5M+ if alumni network compounds.

**Whos.** Cohort-ops Who (FT, Year 1 of D launch); community manager (FT); content/curriculum lead (Mel's natural extension if she likes teaching); franchise compliance only if franchising.

**Risk.** No track record to license — selling methodology requires proof, GVC has months of engagements at launch, not years; founders are not natural teachers/community-leaders (no public evidence Matt or Mel is wired to run a 100-person cohort); methodology gets cloned once curriculum is shared — only network effects (alumni community) are durable; cohort-business commoditization (Reforge, On Deck, every solo creator on Maven — crowded, shrinking margins, 2024–2025 saw consolidation); brand dilution from poorly-delivering franchisees.

**Founder lifestyle.** Heavy content production Year 1, Matt 50+ hr/wk during launch. Mel highly content-and-community-facing — significant on-camera, on-cohort time. Optionality moderate after launch; very low during launch.

**Walk-away value.** Variable. Profitable cohort + community with 1,000+ alumni and recurring revenue sells for 3–5x revenue. Franchise system with 20+ units and royalty stream sells for 5–8x EBITDA. Both require 4+ years.

**Decision points.** M6: have informal "teach me your playbook" requests come unsolicited from 5+ qualified consultants? If yes — D is being market-pulled. If no — premature. M12/24: are alumni of any cohort *actually shipping* GVC-quality work? If no — methodology is non-transferable, or D fails.

**Sullivan/Hardy abundance compatibility.** High *if mature*, low *if premature*. Right Path D is Year 3+; wrong Path D is Year 1.

## Recommendation — for GVC specifically

**Primary: A → C.** Five GVC-specific facts drive the recommendation:

1. **WikiFold already exists.** Most consultants who dream of a SaaS spinoff don't have a built product to fork. Matt does. This collapses Path C's hardest barrier (initial build cost) into a positioning + GTM problem, which is more solvable than a build problem.
2. **Mel's hospitality network is finite, high-conviction.** Large enough to fund 2-person Path A revenue but not large enough to fund Path B's payroll. Exactly right-sized for productized consulting.
3. **Neither founder has demonstrated team-building joy.** The "two humans" wedge is the brand. Path B's success requires founders to *love* what they are publicly framing as not-the-product. Inconsistent.
4. **Sullivan/Hardy "Unique Ability" doctrine.** Matt's UA = systems architecture + product judgment. Mel's UA = operator empathy + trust. Neither UA is consumed by Path A. Both are amplified by A → C. Path B consumes both in management overhead.
5. **Thesis-validation explicitly notes 24–36 month interface-commoditization risk.** GVC has a clean window to convert consulting-earned trust into product-distribution leverage before configuration commoditizes. Path A alone does not survive this; A → C does.

**Credible alternative: Path B.** If, by Month 12, demand is genuinely 3x capacity *and* Mel finds herself wanting to build a hospitality practice with named lieutenants, escalate to B. Respectable but not the asymmetric path. Default A → C; escalate to B only if forced by demand and pulled by founder appetite.

**Reject Path D from zero.** Revisit Year 3+ with 30+ engagements, an alumni-of-clients story, and a brand bigger than the founders.

## Hybrid options — parallel vs exclusive

### Strongly compatible (run in parallel)

- **A + C (recommended primary).** Consulting cash funds product. Consulting reveals product requirements. Product reduces consulting delivery time, expanding consulting margin. Each side compounds the other.
- **A + light D (community, not cohort).** A free or low-cost newsletter/community for SMB-AI-curious owners is *brand expansion* of A, not a separate path. No Path D commitment costs.

### Weakly compatible (possible but creates drag)

- **B + C.** Possible but team scaling pulls capital and attention from product. 37signals managed it because Jason and David protected product time aggressively; most try and fail. Only attempt with discipline.
- **A + early D (cohort).** Premature methodology licensing while still scaling consulting splits founder voice and audience. Park D until Year 3.

### Mutually exclusive

- **B + D.** Both demand high founder time *outside* client delivery. Doing both means doing neither well.
- **C + D simultaneously from zero.** Each demands enough product/content focus that they cannibalize each other's launch energy. Sequence: C first, D once C has paying customers and an alumni-of-customers story.

### Sequencing

```
Year 0–1:  A (lock revenue, productize, sharpen wedge)
             + light brand-expansion (newsletter, Looms, essays)
             + WikiFold runs as internal tool, observe pull

Year 1–2:  A → A+C (fork WikiFold or sibling into SaaS)
             consulting margin stays 70%; SaaS in invest mode

Year 2–3:  A+C with C scaling
             consulting becomes more selective; SaaS revenue passes 30% of total

Year 3–4:  C dominant + selective consulting + optional D launch
             only if alumni-of-clients have real demand to learn the methodology

Year 4+:   Three-arm Self-Multiplying Company —
             consulting (selective, premium, 20% of revenue)
             product (60% of revenue, primary growth engine)
             methodology/network (20% of revenue, brand and IP layer)
```

This sequence respects every constraint visible in the existing materials: Matt and Mel's energy budgets, the operator-engineer-as-product wedge, the Sullivan/Hardy abundance frame, the validated thesis window, and bootstrap-only capital reality. It is the path most likely to deliver both the lifestyle Matt and Mel say they want *and* the asymmetric outcome implied by 10x trajectory thinking.
