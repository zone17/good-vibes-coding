## 1. Positioning statement (one paragraph)

Good Vibes Coding is the AI partner for small businesses that don't want to fire their staff or buy another tool they'll never use. We work with $200K–$5M operators — restaurants, agencies, clinics, studios, service shops the consulting world ignores and the SaaS world flattens — and we make the people already inside those businesses dramatically more capable with AI. Two humans (an engineering leader and a hospitality operator) building the small, sharp tools that turn five-hour weekly chores into five-minute ones. Not headcount cuts. Not a subscription. A judgment partner who knows AI *and* operations, staying until the leverage is real.

## 2. The market frame

Two stories dominate the AI conversation. Both fail the small business owner.

**Big Consulting's "AI to do the same with less people."** Accenture, Deloitte, BCG, McKinsey scope AI engagements around headcount reduction. The math only works for enterprises with hundreds of seats to cut, six-figure minimums, and political appetite for layoffs. A 12-person restaurant group can't afford the deck and wouldn't survive the prescription. *And the prescription is failing* — Klarna's re-hiring, IBM's walkbacks, the Air Canada chatbot ruling. Pure replacement is fragile.

**The SaaS "buy our AI tool" pitch.** ChatGPT Team, Notion AI, HubSpot AI, Intercom Fin, every vertical SaaS bolting "Agent" onto its pricing page. Set-and-forget, no judgment partner, no integration with how the business actually runs. Tool fatigue — not AI excitement — is the dominant emotion in SMB now.

**The third path GVC owns: augmentation, delivered by humans who understand both halves.** ~33M US small businesses won't have an AI budget line, won't hire in-house ML, and have been burned by at least one SaaS. They don't need fewer people. They need the people they have to be 10x more useful with AI in the loop — and someone trustworthy to set it up and stay in the room. That service category doesn't exist at this price band. We're staking it.

## 3. Target ICP refinement

### Primary persona: "Dana, the over-extended owner-operator"

Dana runs a 6–25 person business doing $400K–$3M annual revenue. Three restaurants in West Palm. A 14-person digital agency. A regional HVAC outfit. A boutique law practice. A med spa. She is the bottleneck — quoting, scheduling, invoicing, follow-up, hiring, vendor management, weekend ops fires funnel to her phone. She doesn't want to be a tech company. She wants her Saturdays back.

She has tried: a Zapier setup that broke, a CRM her team won't update, a $400/mo SaaS suite she uses 12% of, ChatGPT for emails, one cousin/freelancer/agency that overpromised and ghosted. *Not* AI-skeptical — AI-fatigued. One bad pitch from never taking another sales call.

**3 trigger moments**

1. **The 10pm reconciliation.** Payroll / inventory / the same export-to-CSV dance she's done for two years. She knows AI should be doing this. Doesn't know who to trust.
2. **The third quote her bookkeeper missed this week.** A client churns or a vendor errors. She can't hire her way out — she has the right people; the *system* is broken.
3. **The forgotten SaaS invoice.** $389/mo, eight months, used twice. $30k+/year on tools her team doesn't use while doing the work those tools were supposed to automate. The "buy more software" story is dead.

**What she Googles, who she asks first**

- Googles: "AI for small business," "AI virtual assistant for [her industry]," "[SaaS she hates] alternative," "fractional CTO small business."
- Asks first: a peer in her owner network (BNI, EO, Vistage, Chamber, industry FB/Slack), her bookkeeper, sometimes her web person. Not LinkedIn thought leaders. Not Hacker News.
- High-signal channels for us: Melissa's hospitality network, peer referrals, one good essay or podcast in a tight community.

**What she's afraid of**

- Replacing herself or her team — *especially* long-tenured staff. Values fear, not just labor-cost. She built this with these people.
- Buying another tool that doesn't get used. Spending $15k and getting a Notion doc.
- Being condescended to by a 25-year-old "AI consultant" who's never run a P&L or worked a Saturday shift.
- Looking dumb — unable to evaluate if the work is good.
- AI doing something embarrassing or illegal in front of a customer (the Air Canada moment).

Replacement fear is real and almost never named on AI consulting sites. Naming it directly is among the highest-leverage moves we have.

## 4. Core message architecture

**One-sentence promise.**
We make the people already inside your business dramatically more capable with AI — without firing anyone, without another subscription, and without you needing to become a tech company to evaluate the work.

**Three proof pillars.**

1. **We don't replace people. We hand them better tools.** *Evidence:* every scope preserves headcount; we publish "five-hours-back-per-person-per-week" math instead of FTE-reduction math. Sample builds show a named team member's task shortened, not eliminated.
2. **An engineer and an operator, not two engineers.** *Evidence:* Matt's named prior engineering orgs + Melissa's named NYC/SoFL venues, LinkedIn-verifiable. Most AI shops are two engineers who've never worked a Saturday dinner service or made payroll on a slow week.
3. **Plain language, published prices, no extraction.** *Evidence:* pricing ranges (pilot, monthly, audit) on the page. Process transparency. A written disqualification block. WikiFold, free, as proof we ship.

**The one thing we explicitly won't do.**
We won't take an engagement whose business case requires firing someone. If your goal is FTE reduction, we'll tell you in the first 10 minutes and point you toward a Big Consulting firm. (Secondary disqualifications: enterprises >$25M revenue; "a chatbot" without a defined job for it; lowest-bid on a fixed spec — that's Fiverr, not partnership.)

## 5. Voice & tone — sharper than current

The current site is on-voice but soft. "We believe technology should lift people up" could appear on 200 other sites. Extending §9:

**5 phrases we *will* use:**
1. "We won't fire your people for you."
2. "Five hours back per person, per week."
3. "Your team, with sharper tools."
4. "If AI isn't the right answer, we'll tell you."
5. "We've made payroll on a slow week. We've shipped at 2am. Both matter here."

**5 phrases we *won't* use:**
1. "AI-powered." (every SaaS vendor says it)
2. "Transform your business." (consultant-deck cliché)
3. "Unlock the power of AI for your team." (template SaaS copy)
4. "Future-proof." (lazy, undefined)
5. "Trusted by leading businesses across [industry]." (logo theater)

Rule: if a line could appear unedited on a Big Consulting deck or a Series-B SaaS landing page, rewrite it in the voice of "two people at your kitchen table explaining what they're going to do."

## 6. Tagline candidates (ranked)

Each ≤8 words. Tested: (a) carries the augmentation frame, (b) sounds like *us*.

1. **"AI for your team, not instead of them."** Plain, contrarian, defensible. Strongest line. **Primary.**
2. **"We make your people 10x. Without replacing them."** "10x" borrowed but rescued by clause two. More swagger than feels native.
3. **"Your team, sharper. Your bills, smaller."** Concrete, two-clause, owner-legible. Doesn't name AI — a feature for AI-fatigued buyers.
4. **"AI that augments. Humans that stay."** Aphoristic — risks consultant-y. Half-step from how they talk.
5. **"Small business AI that doesn't replace small businesses."** Category-defining. Better written than spoken. Best as essay title.

Ship #1 as primary, hold #3 for body-copy/footer. Avoid #4 — closest to consultant-speak.

## 7. Hero section direction

Current H1 — "We build tech that *actually helps people.*" — is warm but generic. Could appear on 50 boutique-dev sites. Doesn't name the audience, AI, or the wedge. Current subhead's "punch way above your weight" is metaphor where specificity converts.

### New H1 candidates

**A (recommended):** AI for your team, not instead of them.
**B (more concrete, more risk):** We make small businesses 10x more capable with AI — without firing anyone.

A is sharper, shorter, carries an editorial typographic hero (Sierra/Anthropic register). B is more explicit but reads slightly like a deck headline.

### New subhead candidates

**A:** We're an engineer and an operator who plug AI into the repetitive work eating your team's week — so the people you already trust get five hours back to do the work only they can do. No headcount cuts. No new subscription. No deck.

**B:** Two humans — 25 years engineering, 20 years hospitality ops — building small, sharp AI tools for $200K–$5M businesses. We won't sell you a SaaS seat. We won't fire your people for you. We'll just make the work easier.

Default to A unless we lean fully into the disqualification frame.

### What the H1 should NOT say

- Anything with "transform," "unlock," "supercharge," "empower," "elevate," "revolutionize," "the power of AI."
- Anything where AI is the subject — *we* and *your team* should be. AI is the tool, not the protagonist.
- Anything that reads as a feature list ("AI automation, custom software, agents") — that's an H2 job.
- Anything hiding behind metaphor ("punch above your weight," "level the playing field"). Owners are tired of metaphor.
- Anything requiring AI literacy to parse. Dana is operator-literate; that's the right asymmetry.

## 8. Risks / honest gaps

### Where this positioning could fail

- **"We won't fire your people" reads as anti-progress** to buyers who *do* want headcount reduction. Trade: lose ~10% of top-of-funnel, gain trust with the 90% we want.
- **Augmentation is harder to quantify than replacement.** "1 FTE = $80k/year" is clean for a CFO. "Five hours back per person, per week" requires the buyer to do the math; many won't. Publish the math in case studies.
- **The frame depends on us shipping augmentation that works.** If first 3–5 engagements produce tools teams don't adopt, the positioning is hollow. Proof stack (sample builds, before/after metrics, testimonials from *team members*, not just the owner) has to be real.
- **"AI consulting for SMB" is crowding.** Every Twitter operator with a Make.com login is an AI consultant now. Our differentiation: engineer + operator combo, published prices, explicit wedge. The category will look generic in 12–18 months.
- **The "two humans" promise strains at scale.** Past 4–6 active engagements, "you work directly with us" gets thin. Decide: small named team (3–6) or cap engagements. Brief assumes cap for next 12 months.

### What would need to be true

1. Matt and Melissa publicly commit to no-headcount-cuts — site, FAQ, calls. Binding constraint, not a flourish.
2. Ship 2–3 sample-build Looms (handoff §6 Batch F U20) legible to an owner without AI vocabulary. "Here's what Maria's Tuesday looked like before. Here's what it looks like now." Not "here's our agent architecture."
3. Pricing ranges get published (handoff §6 Batch A U18). Filters tire-kickers, earns the trust the disqualification block needs.
4. The disqualification block (handoff §6 Batch A U15) actually disqualifies someone real. Theater otherwise.
5. The wedge gets reinforced in one essay, Loom, or podcast in the next 60 days. Positioning dies if it lives only on the homepage.

### Open questions to resolve before site rewrite

- **Q1:** Hard rule or strong default on "no FTE-reduction engagements"? Brief assumes hard rule. *Blocks Hero rewrite.*
- **Q2:** Is "Dana" the right primary persona, or narrow to one industry (hospitality, Melissa's home turf)? *Blocks Hero specificity.*
- **Q3:** Comfortable saying "10x" in copy? Borrowed from VC-speak. Alternative: "five hours back per person, per week." *Blocks tagline lock.*
- **Q4:** Pricing ranges — what are they? Cannot ship without numbers. *Blocked on Matt — handoff §7 item 4.*
- **Q5:** Name competitors (Big Consulting, SaaS vendors) explicitly, or implicitly? Recommend: implicit in H1/subhead, explicit in one essay. *Blocks FAQ copy.*
- **Q6:** Hero visual — the augmentation frame argues for an editorial typographic hero; the line *is* the hero. Confirm before U33 spawns. *Blocks design refresh.*
