## Constraints (anti-patterns from handoff §9 — do not violate)

Every line below was checked against these rules. If you edit anything, re-check.

1. **No invented stats.** No "trusted by 1,000+." No latency brags. The "33M small businesses" figure is a public Census/SBA number; we use it as a market frame, not a customer count.
2. **No stock photography references.** Copy never asks the reader to imagine a "team at a whiteboard."
3. **No logo-cloud theater.** Zero "as featured in," zero unearned partner badges.
4. **No corporate "we."** First-person plural "we." When credibility needs a face, name Matt or Melissa directly. Never "our team of experts."
5. **No SaaS-template vocabulary.** Banned: *unlock, supercharge, revolutionary, cutting-edge, powered by AI, next-gen, game-changing, seamless, world-class, robust, leverage, synergy, transform your business.*
6. **No 2021 visual cues in copy.** Don't reference gradients, glow, neon, glassmorphism, or "modern" as adjective.
7. **Pricing not hidden once Matt commits ranges.** These drafts assume Batch A ships before/with the rewrite — the disqualification block points at price ranges where appropriate.
8. **No vanity conversion tricks.** No urgency, no scarcity, no "limited spots."
9. **No keyword-stuffed SEO copy.** Reads like two humans, not an "ultimate guide."
10. **No fake-code visual references.** The hero copy is written so it stands on its own without the current `const goodVibes = {…}` block — that block should be retired with U33.

**Voice anchors used throughout:** plain words; specific nouns over abstractions; italic for the one beat per heading that earns it; sentences that scan in a single breath; named humans; the truth.

## Variant A — "Don't fire your team. Make them unstoppable."

The provocative one. Plants a flag. Frames AI buying as a moral and operational choice the reader has to make right now.

### Eyebrow
Augmentation, not replacement.

### H1
Don't fire your team.<br>
<em>Make them unstoppable.</em>

### Subhead
We use AI to take the busywork off the people you already trust — so a 4-person shop ships like a 40-person one, without firing anyone or buying ten more SaaS seats.

### Primary CTA
Book a 30-min call

### "Is this for you / Not a fit" block

**Section title:** Is this for you?

**Good fit:**
- You run a business doing roughly $200K–$5M a year.
- You have a small team you actually like, doing too much by hand.
- You're tired of paying for software your people barely use.
- You'd rather make your existing crew 10x more capable than hire three more.

**Not a fit:**
- You're hoping to replace humans wholesale. We won't help with that.
- You're an enterprise looking for a six-month McKinsey deck. Wrong shop.
- You want a chatbot for the sake of having one. We'll talk you out of it.
- You need someone to rubber-stamp a decision you've already made.

### Mission rewrite (~3 paragraphs)

**Section label:** Our take
**Heading:** Two extremes.<br><em>A third path.</em>

There are roughly 33 million small businesses in the US. Almost none of them will ever get on a Big Consulting roadmap, and almost none of them need the kind of AI platform that costs more than their rent. The two loud options in the market right now — six-figure consulting engagements and bolt-on SaaS subscriptions — were built for somebody else.

We started Good Vibes Coding because the people running real businesses deserve a third option. Not a deck. Not another seat license. Real AI work, done shoulder-to-shoulder with the team you already have, priced for a business that has to make payroll on Friday.

Our wedge is simple: *augmentation, not replacement.* We don't help anyone fire their staff. We make the humans already inside a small business 10x more capable — so the bookkeeper closes the month in an afternoon, the ops lead stops copy-pasting between five tools, and the owner gets a Saturday back. That's the whole job.

### Anti-sales reassurance line
No pitch. No pressure. If AI isn't the right answer for your business, we'll tell you in the first ten minutes — and point you at something better.

### Third-path one-liner
The third path between Big Consulting and bolt-on SaaS: AI that makes your existing team unstoppable.

### Voice notes / why this works / where it might miss

*Why this works.* The H1 is a stance, not a feature. "Don't fire your team" preempts the #1 unspoken fear small-business owners have about AI consultants in 2026, which is that you'll show up and tell them to lay off Susan in accounting. Naming the fear and rejecting it is more disarming than any reassurance paragraph. The subhead trades on a concrete shape ("4-person shop ships like a 40-person one") instead of an abstraction.

*Why it might miss.* "Don't fire your team" can land sharp if the reader has *already* let people go in the last year, or is actively planning layoffs unrelated to AI. The provocation also commits us to a position — we genuinely won't take engagements where the explicit goal is replacement — so Matt has to be willing to walk away from those leads. If that's not true, this variant lies.

## Variant B — "AI for the businesses Big Consulting forgot."

The populist one. Quietly confident. Frames GVC by who they're *for* (the underserved 33M) rather than what they reject. Reads more like Basecamp's manifesto pages than a sales site.

### Eyebrow
For the other 33 million.

### H1
AI for the businesses<br>
<em>Big Consulting forgot.</em>

### Subhead
McKinsey won't return your call and a $99/month tool can't run your business. We're the third option — small-shop AI work for owners who'd rather spend less and ship more than sit through another roadmap workshop.

### Primary CTA
Book a 30-min call

### "Is this for you / Not a fit" block

**Section title:** Who this is for

**Good fit:**
- $200K–$5M businesses too small for Deloitte, too real for a SaaS sandbox.
- Owners who like their team and want to give them better tools, not replace them.
- Operators paying for software stacks nobody fully uses anymore.
- Anyone who wants to know what AI is actually good for in *their* business — not in general.

**Not a fit:**
- Fortune 500 procurement. We don't fill out vendor questionnaires.
- "We just need a chatbot." If that's the brief, the answer is usually no.
- Anyone shopping for the cheapest possible bid. We're not that.
- Founders who want a yes-man. We'll disagree with you, kindly, when it matters.

### Mission rewrite (~3 paragraphs)

**Section label:** Our take
**Heading:** The market<br><em>built for someone else.</em>

There are 33 million small businesses in this country. The big consultancies serve maybe the top 0.1% of them. The rest are left choosing between SaaS dashboards built for someone else's workflow and YouTube tutorials that haven't been updated in two years. That's not a market. That's a gap.

We're a two-person shop because we wanted to work in that gap, not above it. Matt has spent 25 years running engineering inside companies you'd recognize. Melissa spent twenty years running the floor at NYC and South Florida hospitality rooms where the margin was three percent and the answer always had to be *yes.* We've both lived inside small businesses being underserved by enterprise tools. Now we build the tools we wished we had.

Our pitch is *augmentation, not replacement.* We don't show up to fire anyone. We show up to make the four or fourteen people you already have move like a much bigger team — automating the manual stuff, replacing the SaaS bills that aren't earning their keep, and handing your operators AI agents that do the part of the job nobody actually liked doing.

### Anti-sales reassurance line
No pitch deck. No pressure. If we're not a fit, we'll say so on the call and send you somewhere that is — usually for free.

### Third-path one-liner
We do AI work for the 33 million small businesses Big Consulting forgot.

### Voice notes / why this works / where it might miss

*Why this works.* The hook is positional rather than ideological — it lands even with a reader who hasn't formed a strong opinion about AI yet, because every small-business owner has felt the "too small for Deloitte, too real for SaaS" squeeze. Naming the gap names *them*. The Mission paragraphs introduce Matt and Melissa with specific texture ("the margin was three percent and the answer always had to be *yes*") rather than year counts. The third-path line is the most quotable of the three and reads cleanly as a footer or OG line.

*Why it might miss.* "Big Consulting forgot" is the warmest of the three positionings but also the least urgent — it doesn't push the reader toward a Calendly slot the way Variant A does. If the site is converting cold visitors who arrived from a paid channel, Variant A out-pulls this. If the site is converting warm referrals (which is most of GVC's actual top of funnel today), this is probably the better fit.

## Variant C — "Real humans. Real AI. Real outcomes."

The warm one. Lets the two-humans truth carry the page. Leans on voice and specifics rather than positioning. Closest in spirit to the existing site, but sharper and outcome-led.

### Eyebrow
Two humans. Real AI.

### H1
Real humans.<br>
Real AI.<br>
<em>Real outcomes.</em>

### Subhead
We're Matt and Melissa. We use AI to give small-business teams their Saturdays back — closing the month in an afternoon, killing the SaaS stack nobody fully uses, and turning four people into a force.

### Primary CTA
Book a 30-min call with Matt

### "Is this for you / Not a fit" block

**Section title:** Before you book

**Good fit:**
- Small business, $200K–$5M, with a team you'd run into a fire for.
- You can name three things eating your week that a sharp ops person could automate.
- You're spending more on software than you'd like and using less of it than you'd admit.
- You want a partner who'll explain everything in plain language and tell you when *not* to build something.

**Not a fit:**
- You want a deck that justifies a decision your board has already made.
- You need someone to manage thirty other vendors. We're hands-on, not a GC.
- You're looking for the cheapest possible build. There are cheaper shops; we're not them.
- You'd rather replace people than train them. That's not the work we do.

### Mission rewrite (~3 paragraphs)

**Section label:** Our take
**Heading:** Two people.<br><em>One straight answer.</em>

We're Matt and Melissa, and Good Vibes Coding is the two of us. Matt has spent 25 years running engineering — platform architecture, AI-native products, the boring infrastructure work that keeps real software alive. Melissa spent twenty years inside high-end NYC and South Florida hospitality, the kind of operation where you learn fast that the only thing that matters is whether the team on the floor can actually deliver.

We started this shop because the AI conversation in 2026 has gone sideways. One side of the market is selling *replace your people.* The other side is selling *another subscription.* The 33 million small businesses in the middle — the ones running on tight teams, slim margins, and people they trained themselves — get neither a useful answer nor a fair price.

Our answer is *augmentation, not replacement.* We come in, learn the actual work, and use AI to take the manual tax off the humans already doing it. Bookkeepers close the month in an afternoon. Ops leads stop copy-pasting between five tools. Owners get a Saturday back. Same team, ten times the leverage. That's the whole job, and we'd rather be honest about it than dress it up.

### Anti-sales reassurance line
No pitch. No pressure. We'll tell you in the first ten minutes if AI is the right move — and we'll tell you when it isn't, because most of what you read online is selling you something either way.

### Third-path one-liner
Real humans, real AI, real outcomes — augmentation for the small businesses the market forgot.

### Voice notes / why this works / where it might miss

*Why this works.* "Real humans. Real AI. Real outcomes." is the most defensible H1 of the three because every word maps to a thing on the page (the team photo, the WikiFold tool, the published process). The Mission rewrite gives the strongest founder voice — Melissa's hospitality detail does more credibility work than any number of years would. "Book a 30-min call with Matt" is the highest-trust CTA we can ship today; it tells the reader exactly who they'll be on Zoom with.

*Why it might miss.* This variant is the least differentiated on positioning. A reader who's already on five "AI consulting for small business" sites this morning will find the H1 generic until they read the subhead — and that's a clickthrough cost. It also shifts more weight onto Matt's name on the CTA, which is great for trust and fragile if Matt's calendar tightens. Worth A/B-able against B as the "warm referral" default.

## Recommendation

Ship **Variant A** as the primary draft for Matt's review, with Variant B's Mission section borrowed as a swap option. Here's the honest tradeoff.

Variant A is the only one that *commits to a position the market hasn't claimed yet.* "Augmentation, not replacement" is the wedge in the handoff brief, and A is the only variant where the H1, the disqualification block, and the Mission paragraphs all hold the same line at once. The provocation in "Don't fire your team" will repel some buyers — but those are buyers who would have wasted a call anyway, which is exactly what U15 is for. A is the version where the page does the qualification work *before* the form does.

Variant B has the strongest *single line* ("AI for the businesses Big Consulting forgot") and the best Mission paragraphs — the "33 million" framing earns attention without sounding like a stat we invented. If Matt finds A too sharp, don't soften A: ship B's hero with A's disqualification block and anti-sales line. That hybrid keeps the position without leading with the punch. Variant C is what GVC ships if the audience is "warm referrals only" — safest, warmest, compounds least. Don't ship C alone. Use its third-path one-liner in the footer and OG card regardless of which hero we pick.

Every variant assumes Batch A (anti-sales CTA + disqualification block + pricing scaffold) ships in the same PR as the hero rewrite. A sharper hero without the disqualification block under it leaves the page louder *and* less honest. If only one piece ships this week, ship the disqualification block — the hero can wait.
