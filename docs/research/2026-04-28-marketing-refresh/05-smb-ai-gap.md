## TL;DR

The US has **36.2 million small businesses** (SBA Office of Advocacy, Jun 2025) — the "33M" figure is slightly dated but the positioning logic is sound and has grown stronger. The gap is not adoption rate on paper (76% of small business owners report using AI per Goldman Sachs, Feb 2026) but **integration depth**: only 14% have fully embedded AI into core operations, and 73% say they need more training and resources to do so. The dominant barrier is not cost or tool availability — it is the absence of a trusted human advisor who can translate AI capability into operational reality for an owner already drowning in SaaS subscriptions they don't fully use.

---

## Market sizing

- **Exact SMB count:** 36.2 million small businesses in the US (SBA Office of Advocacy, 2025 Small Business Profiles, Jun 2025). This is up from 34.8M in the Nov 2024 report. The "33M" figure circulating in earlier marketing copy is from the 2023 data vintage.
- **Employer vs. nonemployer split:**
  - ~30.4 million nonemployer businesses (sole proprietors, single-member LLCs, independent contractors) — roughly 84% of all small businesses
  - ~5.5–6 million employer firms (at least one paid employee) — ~16%
  - Small businesses represent 99.9% of all US businesses and ~46% of private-sector employment
- **Breakdown by employee size (employer firms):**
  - Firms with 1–9 employees: majority of employer small businesses; NFIB 2025 survey shows 55% AI familiarity at this tier, vs. 48% for nonemployers and 82% for 50+ employee firms
  - Firms with 50+ employees: 48% AI adoption rate (NFIB, 2025)
  - Firms with 10–100 employees: jumped from 47% to 68% AI adoption 2024–2025 (Thryv, Jul 2025)
- **Revenue breakdown:** US Census Bureau SUSB and Economic Census data are employee-centric, not revenue-centric at fine-grained bands. JP Morgan Chase Institute analysis (2025) uses a proxy: firms with >$250K revenue show 22.5% AI adoption vs. 15.1% for firms ≤$250K revenue — confirming that the sub-$250K tier is the least-served. GVC's stated target band of $200K–$5M revenue spans both cohorts, capturing the lower-adoption end as an acquisition pool and the higher-adoption end (already experimenting but stuck) as the conversion-ready buyer.
- **Source / methodology:** SBA Office of Advocacy synthesizes US Census Bureau Statistics of U.S. Businesses (SUSB), Bureau of Labor Statistics, and Annual Business Survey data. The 2025 profiles use 2022–2024 vintage Census data with job-creation data through March 2024. The 36.2M figure is the most current authoritative count available.

**Key methodological note:** The spread between 8.8% (Census production definition) and 76% (Goldman Sachs self-report) is not a contradiction — it reflects what question is asked. "Do you use ChatGPT sometimes?" gets 76%. "Is AI integrated into how you produce your product or service?" gets 8.8%. GVC's opportunity lives in the gap between those two numbers: the ~60% of owners who dabble but haven't integrated.

## Top pain points (cited by small biz owners)

- **Lack of technical expertise / skills gap** — 40% of SMBs cite this as primary barrier (OECD SME AI Adoption Report, Dec 2025); 63% of employers cite skills gap (World Economic Forum, 2025); only 27% of small business owners feel confident adopting AI effectively vs. 82% of mid-sized firms (Forbes/SMB Group).
- **Don't know where to start / tool overwhelm** — The tools landscape is crowded and "vendor claims are difficult to evaluate without technical expertise" (AI Journal, 2026). Average company now runs 7 overlapping AI app subscriptions performing redundant tasks (BetterCloud State of SaaS, 2026). 48% of Goldman Sachs survey respondents cited "difficulty choosing the right AI tools" (Feb 2026).
- **Data privacy and security concerns** — 50% of AI-using small firms worry about data security (Goldman Sachs, Feb 2026); 65% of small businesses worry new AI regulations could harm their operations, up 11 percentage points year-over-year (US Chamber, Aug 2025).
- **Insufficient budget / unclear ROI** — 40% cite budget as barrier (multiple surveys); only 16% of small businesses spend more than $150/month on AI tools (JP Morgan Chase Institute, 2025); median monthly spend was $28 in 2025, indicating most are on free tiers without meaningful deployment.
- **Belief that AI doesn't apply to their business** — 82% of very small firms (under 5 employees) cite this as primary reason for non-adoption (SBA Office of Advocacy). This is a messaging/education problem, not a product problem.
- **Lack of training and resources** — 73% of small business owners say more training and resources would help them successfully implement AI (Goldman Sachs, Feb 2026). Only 12% invest in AI training despite 29% citing lack of training as an obstacle (OECD, 2025).
- **Software/SaaS fatigue as compounding factor** — Average company already wastes 17–25% of its software budget on unused tools (Zylo, 2026). AI tools are now the fastest-growing source of redundant SaaS sprawl. Owners are not looking for more tools — they are looking for someone to make sense of what they already have.
- **Fear of replacing staff** — A secondary but real concern: 87% of small businesses using AI say it augments rather than displaces employees (Goldman Sachs, Feb 2026), but this fear is a buying objection that must be addressed upfront in messaging.

## Competitive landscape (who else serves SMBs)

| Competitor type | Examples | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Big Four / Global Strategy | Deloitte Digital, Accenture | Deep resources, credibility | Minimums $250K+; irrelevant to sub-$5M revenue businesses |
| Mid-Market AI Boutiques | Xcelacore, AI REV, Vstorm | Domain expertise, structured methodology | $50K–$250K project minimums; target $50M+ companies; no SMB play |
| Tool-first Platforms (no-code chatbot builders) | Botpress ($89–$495/mo), Botsify, Quickchat, Yellow.ai | Low cost, self-serve | No human advisory layer; owners buy and then don't know what to do; high churn |
| Indie SMB AI Consultants | Lifebots.Co, DarwinApps, BridgeView IT | Accessible price point; diagnosis-first positioning | 1–3 person shops; limited vertical expertise; no dual-founder story; no ops credibility |
| Government / Nonprofit | SBDCs (now AI-funded), SCORE, Women's Business Centers | Free; trusted; national reach | Education only — no implementation; curriculum-based not relationship-based |
| Regional digital agencies | Hundreds of local web/marketing shops now adding "AI services" | Existing client relationships | AI is an add-on feature, not a practice; no engineering depth; no strategic advisory |
| Vertical SaaS with AI features baked in | Toast (restaurants), Mindbody (wellness), ServiceTitan (trades) | Pre-integrated; no-lift for owner | Single vertical; no cross-functional advisory; owner still lacks strategy across the stack |

**White space summary:** No competitor at sub-$50K engagement price offers (a) a dual-founder team with both engineering depth AND operational domain expertise, (b) a relationship-first retainer model (not project-based), (c) explicit targeting of the software-fatigued owner who wants one team to talk to across all AI questions. The closest is the indie consultant tier, which lacks the operational credibility (Melissa's 20 years of hospitality ops) and the structured methodology.

## Implications for GVC positioning

- **The "33M" frame needs an update, but the thesis is stronger.** The number is now 36.2 million (SBA, Jun 2025). More importantly, the gap between "dabbling" and "integrated" is now empirically documented: 76% claim use, 8.8%–17.7% actually have operational integration, 14% are fully embedded. GVC's value proposition lives precisely in that chasm. Update all marketing copy to use "36M" and emphasize the integration gap, not just the adoption gap.

- **"Trusted advisor" is the exact gap the market has named.** Goldman Sachs (Feb 2026), the AI for Main Street Act (Apr 2026), and the NFIB (Jun 2025) all converge on the same finding: owners want human guidance, not more tools. GVC's "one team to talk to" positioning is not a differentiator — it is the category definition. Lean into it explicitly. The category is "AI advisory for main street businesses," and GVC should own that phrase.

- **The sub-5-employee nonemployer tier (30.4M of the 36.2M) is NOT GVC's buyer.** JP Morgan Chase Institute data confirms that the $200K–$5M revenue band (which overlaps with employer firms of 1–50 employees) is the operational sweet spot. The 30.4M nonemployers mostly lack the operational complexity that generates ROI for an advisory engagement. GVC's ICP refinement: employer firms with 2–50 employees, $200K–$5M revenue, already have some AI tools they're not fully using. This narrows the addressable market from 36.2M to roughly 3–5M firms — still enormous, far more convertible.

- **Melissa's hospitality/ops background is a primary competitive moat, not just a credential.** The competitive landscape shows zero credible competitors targeting hospitality, F&B, wellness, and service businesses with dual engineering + operational advisory. Vertical SaaS (Toast, Mindbody) owns the tool layer in these verticals but creates no strategic advisory. GVC can position as the "AI advisory layer above your vertical SaaS" for service-industry owners — a specific, defensible niche.

- **SaaS fatigue is the emotional hook, not AI excitement.** The data shows the average small business already wastes 17–25% of its software budget on unused tools and now has 7 overlapping AI subscriptions. Messaging that leads with "we help you figure out what you already have before buying anything new" will resonate more strongly than "AI is the future." The GVC brand's anti-sales, plain-language posture is a structural advantage here — it signals the opposite of the vendor pitch owners are fatigued by.

- **The AI for Main Street Act (2026) is a market tailwind, not a competitor.** SBDC/SCORE programs funded by this legislation will educate small business owners about AI at scale — creating warm demand for implementation help that federal programs cannot provide. GVC should monitor SBDC program rollout and position as the private-sector complement to free government training: "Once you've taken the class, call us to actually do it."

- **Pricing signal: $28/month median AI spend means the bar for "paid advisory" is psychological, not financial.** The JP Morgan data showing $28/month median spend means most SMB owners have not yet made a real financial commitment to AI. The jump from $28/month tool subscriptions to a $1,500–$3,000/month retainer is a category shift, not a price negotiation. GVC's messaging needs to justify why advisory value is different from tool cost — frame it as "the cost of getting AI wrong" (staff time wasted, tools that don't integrate, competitive disadvantage) versus "the cost of getting it right."
